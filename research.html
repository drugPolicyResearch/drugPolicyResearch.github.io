Policymaking

The Misdemeanor Marijuana Diversion Program took effect on March 1, 2017.
- Applies to people 17 and older facing no additional charges other than misdemeanor marijuana possession, county and city law enforcement officials said.
- Decriminalizes low-level marijuana possession, with officials saying on Thursday throwing people in jail for having small amounts of pot had “no tangible public safety benefit.”
- Means in most circumstances there will be no jail, no tickets, no court appearances and no criminal record for possession of less than 4 ounces (113 grams) of marijuana in the city and its surrounding county, home to about 4.5 million people.
- Under Ogg’s new Marijuana Misdemeanor Diversion Program, anyone caught with four ounces or less of marijuana is given the opportunity to attend a drug education course in exchange for their possession charges being dropped. Marijuana is technically still illegal though.

Social Problems Work

Drug addiction is a social problem. There is a definite link between crime and drug addiction. There are many who believe that treatment for drug addiction should be offered to all those affected. Many others though believe that drug addiction should be treated as a health problem and not a criminal issue. With the right treatment, those affected by addiction can overcome their illnesses and go on to be productive members of society. They will cease to be a drain on society and will start to actively contribute to it instead.

Source: https://www.recovery.org.uk/drug-addiction/guide-drug-addiction-social-problem/

Policy Outcomes

People call for additional changes to reduce drug incarceration, and many states are gravitating towards the legalization of medical and recreational marijuana. However, opponents are still fighting for a greater war on drugs. According to the DA’s office, through mid-May, nearly 5,200 people had been diverted from the courts through the Misdemeanor Marijuana Program.

Statistics & Analysis

Data Takeaways




Evidently, prisons and jails have a significant number of individuals who have been incarcerated for drug related offenses. If states are in favor of drug decriminalization, prison and jail populations could be significantly reduced.

This leaves us to question:
- Is incarceration an effective means of dealing with this issue?
- Should we regard marijuana possession and use as crime?






Incarceration rates have increased per capita over the past few decades, suggesting that more individuals are engaging in drug use. This begs to question whether we are implementing policies that are effectively handling the degree of drug use and are sending the right message.




On the left we see both the pros and cons for drug legalization. Those for legalization primarily emphasize the medicinal benefits and consider marijuana to not be very dangerous. Majority of those against legalization believe that legal marijuana would hurt society and individuals and regard marijuana as a dangerous, addictive drug.

When we create polarize this social issue, it results in two arguments that cannot coexist. To make progress on policies that are best for our society, we need to take a better look at modern examples of successful implementations and better understand the complexities of how it affects us as a society.

Recommended Future Policies & Conclusion

For the people who are arrested on drug possession charges or drug related crimes like theft in connection to supporting a drug habit, some need drug treatment, and some need other kinds of services. For the people who are arrested for these kinds of things, they often don’t have health care or an identification card. These are barriers that make it difficult to get other things. We also need services to address other needs.

SS and disability assistance, employment and housings, and mental health treatment is a really big issue that needs to be addressed. People have to go to many places for all these different needs. Those who don’t have reliable transportation can’t afford to do so. We need a one stop shops for all these things.

On the state level, governments should reduce penalty for possession. Currently, less than a gram of marijuana is a felony. This should be a misdemeanor in order for local jurisdiction to have discretion over how they handle it. If it was a misdemeanor then they could set up a diversion program. On the local level, addressing drug use requires much longer term investment in education. There should also be assistance in opioid overdoses use, and needle exchange programs.

Of course, there are downsides to these recommended policies. For example, if people don’t get arrested while they’re under probation, you end up with more people under probation, which widens the net of people under government surveillance. Also, the rise of fines instead of jail times criminalizes being poor because those who are poor cannot pay for the fines. This show that there is still a lot to be done in regards to drug policies.

While legalizing marijuana will likely not happen in Texas for a few years, there is still a lot to discuss about drug policies and drug use. With the opioid epidemic, people are increasingly recognizing that drug treatment is important and evidence-based drug treatment is effective. We are optimistic that Texas is heading in the direction of more drug reform.
