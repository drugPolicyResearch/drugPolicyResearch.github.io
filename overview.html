Overview

This is what you need to know

What is drug decriminalization and legalization?

First, it's important to differentiate decriminalization and legalization. Drug decriminalization is the elimination of criminal penalities for drug use and possession. This generally includes:

  • Removing penalties for personal drug use
  • Removing penalties for small or large amounts of possession
  • Allowing the possession of equipment for drug use (i.e. syringes)
  • Disregarding low-level drug sales

Drug legalization pertains to the elimination of law limiting drug use, possession, and sale. States may choose to only legalize certain stages in the pipeline, among the following:

  • Legalizing medical use
  • Legalizing recreational use
  • Removing limits on possession
  • Legalizing manufacturing and sale


Drug decriminalization and legalization is becoming more widely accepted.

Over the past 50 years, there has been a surge in global opinions on drug decriminalization. Many countries have made pushes towards progressive drug policy. Among these countries are:

  • Canada, legalized recreational marijuana in October 2018
  • Portugal, which was the first country to decriminalize all drugs
  • Uruguay, legalized marijuana completely in December 2013

Currently in the United States, 10 states and the District of Columbia have legalized recreational use of marijuana and 33 have legalized medical marijuana. Drug legalization is becoming more widely accepted, with 62% of surveyed Americans supporting the legalization of marijuana according to a 2018 Pew Research Center survey.

Each of these countries has had varying success with their progressive policies, but undoubtedly there seems to be an emerging trend of countries adopting more progressive drug laws.


Certain groups of people fall victim to drug laws.

Yet, despite this rising acceptance, more than 2 million people, predominantly African American, are currently incarcerated for possession of small amounts of marijuana. According to a 2018 report by prisonpolicy.org, 1 in 5 incarcerated people is locked up for a drug offense.

While there are many cultural stereotypes about income and addiction, such as that primarily the poor are victims of substance abuse and that the wealthy who are addicted to drugs are morally weak, drug abuse can be found in all socioeconomic classes and income levels for a variety of reasons. This fact begs the idea that socioeconomic class needs to be taken into account whenever considering the implications of a potential drug law.


These are the most relevant issues to drug policy.

Law Enforcement: Usually one of the more innocuous issue topics, the influence that drug legalization and decriminalization have on Law Enforcement is immediate. Although is obvious that both legalization and decriminalization affect Law Enforcement through the new legality of certain substances, but this issue has further reaching, more evasive consequences. A few of these implications are:

  • Public spending / allocation of taxpayer money
  • Influence of drug cartels
  • Incarceration and criminal charges, and its effect on socioeconomic class

Healthcare: Since the longterm health effects of certain substances are the target of current research, both the lack of knowledge and the observed health effects of each of these subsstances is often used as talking points in many drug legislation debates.

Economy: Specifically, legalization of certain substances (mainly marijuana) may have an impact on the nation's economy. This many be due to the fact that legalization will permit the purchase and sale of previously illegal substances creating new markets in the US.

Poverty: A large part of the drug legistlation debate is the effect that certain policies will have on various socioeconomic classes. Particularly, the most used socioeconomic class to debate over drug legislation is low-income, and often debate centers around how propsoed legislation will impact the lives of those less privileged.


There are two competing narratives over more drug legislation.

Essentially, there are two sides to every drug legislation debate: those who support the advancement of more lenient policy, and those who are proponents of the status quo. Some of their viewpoints are given below:

Pro-lenient Policy Proponents:

  1. Decriminalization of certain substances (specifically marijuana), would reduce public spending.
  2. Decriminalization and legalization would lead to the reduction of crime rates.
  3. Legalization would result in an increase in economic output of the nation.

Pro-status quo Policy Proponents:

  1. Decriminalization of certain substances (specifically marijuana), would increase public spending.
  2. Decriminalization and legalization would lead to the increase of crime rates.
  3. Decriminalizing certain substances would have negative effects on the health of different parts of the population.

It is crucial to recognize at this point that each side uses evidence to claim the issues from the previous section to justify their stance. For example, a Pro-lenient Policy supporter will say that decriminalization will lead to reduction time and money spent in law enforcement, thereby reducing public spending. A status quo supporter would rebut this by claiming that drug users will flood to areas with decriminalization legislation, causing a strain on city infrastructure, thereby increasing public spending.
