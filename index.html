about us

We seek to explore the effect of drug laws on various factors of Houston society and how any variance between drug laws and their effect on societal factors might reflect a greater, pervasive issue of the inequality between socioeconomic classes.

We plan on applying a diversified set of models and perspectives in understanding both the purpose and non-purpose of drug laws as it pertains to America’s socioeconomic demographics.

  • Dr. Murguia

    Professor at TAMU specializing in Drug Use

    There is a psychological factor for drug use. People are at the risk of turning to drugs when they are dissatisfied with some aspects of their life. Dr. Murguia stated that he supports the decriminalization and legalization of marijuana because it’s a pharmaceutical drug. He commented that drug laws are more strict on minorities, as African-Americans and Mexican-Americans have it particularly harder in Texas. Of particular note, Dr. Murguia believes that states function as “experiments,” so if a drug policy passed in California ends up being successful, then other states are likely to execute the same thing.


  • Franz Brotzen

    President of Rice Democrats

    Franz thinks that our current policy seems like a better prioritization of resources, not to use attorneys’ time prosecuting these cases and instead going after people who committed assault, robbery, where there is a real victim in the equation. In the years of Devon Anderson and her predecessors, people would be prosecuted on minor charges, possession, which disproportionally affected people of color and with socioeconomically challenged background. Franz stated that there is an inherent bias towards finding people with lower socioeconomic background guilty of crimes because that’s where more crimes exist. And a lot of times for misdemeanors, it’s a matter of where the action takes place. When it comes to drug legislation in Houston, Franz believed that US needs to turn to the fact that we fought and the war on drugs, and it resulted in inhumane treatment non-violent people and did not decrease drug usage. Policies like STAR Court program need to be adopted on a wider scale, and decriminalization should go beyond just marijuana. Franz concluded by warning that despite the good purposes of the policies, a good implementation, which includes proper regulation and oversight, is needed. In the end, it’s just a matter of getting into the nitty-gritty of how we make sure that the drug industry is regulated just like the pharmaceutical industry.
